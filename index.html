The Problem

An information gap that costs years of integration

When immigrants arrive in the Netherlands, they face a painful paradox. Dutch news is everywhere — but impenetrable without language skills. Native-language media is understandable — but disconnected from Dutch reality.

This leaves newcomers in an information vacuum: unable to participate civically, unaware of local systems and rights, and excluded from the very conversations that shape their future.

Research consistently shows this gap delays language acquisition, civic engagement, and economic inclusion — not by months, but by years.

⏱ Typical integration timeline without support

Arrival 6 months 1 year 2+ years: civic integration exam

Language barrier persists through most of this journey without daily structured exposure.

🗞️

Dutch news: accessible but incomprehensible

Mainstream Dutch media requires B2+ language level. Most new arrivals are at A0–A1 when they land, creating an immediate wall between them and local information.

📡

Native media: understandable but disconnected

Home-country news doesn't cover local Dutch politics, housing policy, or healthcare systems — the information immigrants most urgently need.

📉

Civic exclusion and misinformation

Without reliable local information, immigrants are more vulnerable to misinformation and less able to participate in civic life — voting, appealing decisions, accessing services.

🎓

Language learning lacks context

Most language apps use artificial scenarios. Real integration requires real Dutch content — the same news your neighbors discuss every morning.

Evidence-Based Design

Built on peer-reviewed research

StepIn is not an intuition. It is grounded in over a decade of academic findings on immigrant media use, language acquisition, and integration outcomes.

Host-language media use is associated with higher language proficiency, increased preference for host-language interaction, and greater political knowledge.
Dalisay (2012) — Communication Research Reports
Immigrants actively use host-country news to understand political systems and cultural norms, indicating its role as a tool for deliberate integration.
Alencar & Deuze (2017) — European Journal of Communication
Native-language media can facilitate integration by providing culturally accessible information about opportunities and social systems in the host country.
Zhou & Cai (2002) — Qualitative Sociology
Host-language competence enables access to cultural norms and facilitates social interaction — making language proficiency a core driver of integration.
Miglietta & Tartaglia (2009) — Cross-Cultural Research
Language proficiency in the host country language is associated with higher levels of civic participation.
Boyd (2012) — International Journal of Canadian Studies
Exposure to mainstream news media positively predicts participation in civic activities within the host society.
Seo & Moon (2013) — Mass Communication and Society
